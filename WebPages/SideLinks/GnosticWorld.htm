"The heliacal rising of Sirius, the Dog Star, was extremely important to the Egyptians. During one period in their history, the heliacal rising of Sirius occurred at the same time as the Summer solstice and, by coincidence, at the same time as the Nile inundation.

The time is late spring in ancient Egypt. Sirius has risen in the East, across the Nile from the pyramids; but the Sun is already up. Sirius, therefore, cannot be seen when it rises.

The motion of the Earth in its orbit (around the Sun) makes the Sun appear to move East with respect to the stars. A few weeks before the Summer solstice the Sun rises when Sirius rises, but the glare of the dawn is still too bright to allow Sirius to be seen.

Finally the sun has moved far enough to the East of Sirius to permit Sirius to be seen for the first time this year in the pre-dawn sky. By coincidence it is the Summer solstice. This heliacal rising of Sirius roughly coincides with the flooding of the Nile, and for the Egyptians this association of Star, River, and Summer solstice was of great importance." In Search of Ancient Astronomies, by E. C. Krupp, p.32-35.
